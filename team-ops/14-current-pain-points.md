# 14 — Current Pain Points

**Friction points identified from team communications. Not about blame — about fixing systems.**

Last updated: 2026-03-18

---

## 1. {{OWNER_NAME}}son's Reporting Structure Tension

**What happened:** I told {{OPS_ADMIN_NAME}} that {{OWNER_NAME}}son's EODs would be combined with mine. {{OPS_PARTNER_NAME}} asked "Why not just have you both do them?" I responded: "I see no reason why {{OWNER_NAME}}son should report to BlitzMetrics Operations if he works solely for {{COMPANY_NAME}}."

**Root cause:** The boundary between {{COMPANY}} operations and BM/LSS operations isn't clear enough. {{OWNER_NAME}}son works for {{COMPANY}}, but the EOD system is a BM/LSS process. I'm asserting {{COMPANY}}'s independence, which is the right call — but it created confusion because the boundary wasn't documented.

**Fix:** {{OWNER_NAME}}son submits his own EODs to Basecamp but reports to me only. His work gets included in the {{COMPANY}} section of standup, not tracked under BM ops. This is documented in the Team Directory (01) and Decision Rights Matrix (03).

## 2. LSS Budget Constraints / Tool Access

**What happened:** {{OPS_ADMIN_NAME}} told me "LSS is currently facing budget constraints, and we are not able to cover additional tool subscriptions." {{OWNER_NAME}}son and I couldn't access Descript when we needed it.

**Root cause:** {{COMPANY}} has been relying on shared LSS/BM infrastructure, but LSS is — in {{ADVISOR_NAME}}'s own words — "functionally bankrupt." That means our tool access is dependent on an entity that can't reliably pay for tools. That's a fragile setup.

**Fix:** {{COMPANY}} needs its own subscriptions for essential tools. Descript, any SEO tools, anything we need daily. Budget these as {{COMPANY}} operating expenses, not LSS shared costs. If LSS can cover something, great — but we can't depend on it. See Tool Stack (09) for the full list.

## 3. Client Priority vs Personal Brand

**What happened:** {{ADVISOR_NAME}} noted in my EOD feedback: "Take care of clients first, since without them, we have no revenue. Selecting swag and working on our personal brands is alluring, but retention is far more important."

**Root cause:** Fair point. I was splitting time between revenue-generating client work and personal brand building. When you're running a small agency, client delivery has to come first — the personal brand compounds over time, but only if clients are happy enough to stay.

**Fix:** Use the Delegation Framework (05). Personal brand content production goes to {{OWNER_NAME}}son. I focus on client delivery and sales. My personal brand gets built from client results and case studies, not from separate content creation sessions.

## 4. {{OWNER_NAME}}son Hire Qualification Pushback

**What happened:** {{ADVISOR_NAME}} pushed back on {{OWNER_NAME}}son being hired without going through the standard qualifying process (repurpose a Marketing Mechanic video). Then {{ADVISOR_NAME}} and {{OPS_PARTNER_NAME}} flagged AI language in {{OWNER_NAME}}son's initial article.

**Root cause:** I hired a friend quickly because I needed help. {{ADVISOR_NAME}} was right to push back — qualifying standards exist for a reason, and skipping them created a quality risk that immediately showed up.

**Fix:** Going forward, every hire completes a qualifying task reviewed by {{ADVISOR_NAME}}/{{OPS_PARTNER_NAME}} before starting paid work. No exceptions. For {{OWNER_NAME}}son specifically: his quality is being monitored closely for 60 days with explicit benchmarks. This is documented in the Scaling Plan (13) as a non-negotiable hiring standard.

## 5. Financial Transparency Gap

**What happened:** {{ADVISOR_NAME}} noted that I initially didn't share compensation details. He's been pushing for formal P&L reporting and expense accounting before any distributions happen.

**Root cause:** No formal financial reporting system was in place. Revenue flows to me first, then gets distributed — but without clear documentation, that creates trust issues even when everything is above board.

**Fix:** {{OPS_ADMIN_NAME}} produces a monthly P&L per the KP Sprint doc structure. I share financials quarterly with equity holders per the information rights clause in the equity agreement. Transparency eliminates questions before they become friction.

---

## 6. Email vs Basecamp Drift

**What happened:** I've been forwarding tasks to {{OWNER_NAME}}son via email instead of assigning them in Basecamp. {{ADVISOR_NAME}} has repeatedly asked the team to move discussions to Basecamp.

**Root cause:** Email is faster for quick delegation. But it creates information silos — {{ADVISOR_NAME}} and {{OPS_PARTNER_NAME}} can't see what {{OWNER_NAME}}son is working on, blockers don't get flagged in the right place, and tasks fall through the cracks.

**Fix:** All task assignments go through Basecamp. Email is only for credentials and 1:1 coordination that doesn't need team visibility. I need to add {{OWNER_NAME}}son to relevant Basecamp projects and assign tasks there. This is part of the Communication Protocol (02).

## 7. Refund Request (Ed Strachar)

**What happened:** Ed Strachar emailed asking about a $1,500 refund. {{OPS_ADMIN_NAME}} looped the team.

**Root cause:** The specifics vary, but the bigger issue is that we need clear, documented refund policy enforcement so these don't become ad hoc decisions every time.

**Fix:** The 7-day cooling off policy is now documented in client agreements. Process: {{OPS_ADMIN_NAME}} handles initial inquiry, escalates to me + {{ADVISOR_NAME}} if unresolved, we decide together. See Escalation Playbook (12) for the full refund workflow.

## 8. My Own Low Output

**What happened:** I wrote in February 2026: "It applies to me because my output has been so poor, that no matter how good the tool is, there will be no result without action. I am working hard to change that."

**Root cause:** Multiple priorities pulling in different directions — military service obligations, onboarding {{OWNER_NAME}}son, building systems, personal matters. Spread thin across too many things, executing on too few.

**Fix:** The delegation framework is the answer. {{OWNER_NAME}}son handles content production. {{OPS_ADMIN_NAME}} handles admin. The Mainframe handles systems documentation. My time allocation should be: 60% client delivery, 20% sales, 20% strategic. Everything else gets delegated or deferred.

## 9. Google Ads Policy Rejections

**What happened:** Multiple ads got disapproved by Google Ads policy. Time wasted on revisions and resubmissions.

**Root cause:** Ad copy and targeting weren't being reviewed against Google's policies before submission. We were learning by rejection instead of by preparation.

**Fix:** Review all ad copy against Google's current policies before submitting. Assign someone ({{OWNER_NAME}}son or a future content writer) to monitor ad account health weekly. Build a pre-submission checklist based on the most common rejection reasons we've hit.

---

## 10. Shared Tool Access Fragility

**What happened:** The Jumper Media dashboard had an unknown admin. Nobody on the team could identify who set it up. The entire team did a "not me" email chain — nobody claimed it.

**Root cause:** No centralized documentation of who owns what tool, who has admin access, and what credentials exist. When tools are set up ad hoc and people leave or forget, access becomes a mystery.

**Fix:** The Tool Stack document (09) now includes admin/owner info for every tool. {{OPS_ADMIN_NAME}} maintains a master access registry. Any time a new tool is added or access changes, it gets updated immediately. No more "who set this up?" conversations.

## 11. No CRM or Deal Tracking

**What happened:** Client and prospect information lives in scattered emails and Basecamp projects. No centralized pipeline view.

**Root cause:** No system was set up for tracking prospects through qualification to close. Tasks and follow-ups get lost.

**Fix:** Build a lightweight deal tracker in Basecamp or a spreadsheet. Track prospect name, stage, next action, owner, and deadline. Prospect Follow-Up SOP (now created) provides the cadence.

---

## 12. No Content Publishing Calendar

**What happened:** Articles, podcasts, and social content are produced but there is no visible publishing schedule or distribution tracking.

**Root cause:** The pipeline optimized for production volume without a corresponding system for scheduling and verifying publication.

**Fix:** Create a publishing calendar that tracks what was produced, when it was published, and on which platform. Ties into the WordPress API gap identified in the honest assessment.

---

## 13. No Engagement Monitoring for AI Apprentices

**What happened:** Variable participation across apprentices (Alea slow, Morgan inactive) with no systematic check-in cadence or engagement triggers.

**Root cause:** Onboarding exists but ongoing engagement monitoring does not. Apprentices drift without structured accountability.

**Fix:** Weekly engagement check: did they submit work? Did they attend Thursday sessions? If two consecutive misses, trigger outreach. AI Apprentice Onboarding SOP now exists but needs an ongoing engagement section.

## Pattern Summary

Most of these pain points trace back to three root causes:

1. **Unclear boundaries between {{COMPANY}} and BM/LSS.** Fix: Document {{COMPANY}}'s independence, own our tools, own our reporting.
2. **{{OWNER_NAME}} doing too many things.** Fix: Delegation framework, hire a PM, focus on highest-value work.
3. **Communication happening in the wrong channels.** Fix: Basecamp for everything, email for nothing operational.

Fix these three things and 80% of the friction disappears.
