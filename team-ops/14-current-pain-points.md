# 14 — Current Pain Points

**Friction points identified from team communications. Not about blame — about fixing systems.**

Last updated: 2026-03-18

---

## 1. [Your Content Specialist]'s Reporting Structure Tension

**What happened:** I told [Your Ops Admin] that [Your Content Specialist]'s EODs would be combined with mine. [Your Ops Partner] asked "Why not just have you both do them?" I responded: "I see no reason why [Your Content Specialist] should report to [Methodology Partner] Operations if he works solely for [Your Agency Name]."

**Root cause:** The boundary between [Your Agency] operations and [Methodology Partner]/[Partner Platform] operations isn't clear enough. [Your Content Specialist] works for [Your Agency], but the EOD system is a [Methodology Partner]/[Partner Platform] process. I'm asserting [Your Agency]'s independence, which is the right call — but it created confusion because the boundary wasn't documented.

**Fix:** [Your Content Specialist] submits his own EODs to Basecamp but reports to me only. His work gets included in the [Your Agency] section of standup, not tracked under [Methodology Partner] ops. This is documented in the Team Directory (01) and Decision Rights Matrix (03).

---

## 2. [Partner Platform] Budget Constraints / Tool Access

**What happened:** [Your Ops Admin] told me "[Partner Platform] is currently facing budget constraints, and we are not able to cover additional tool subscriptions." [Your Content Specialist] and I couldn't access Descript when we needed it.

**Root cause:** [Your Agency] has been relying on shared [Partner Platform]/[Methodology Partner] infrastructure, but [Partner Platform] is — in [Your Mentor/Advisor]'s own words — "functionally bankrupt." That means our tool access is dependent on an entity that can't reliably pay for tools. That's a fragile setup.

**Fix:** [Your Agency] needs its own subscriptions for essential tools. Descript, any SEO tools, anything we need daily. Budget these as [Your Agency] operating expenses, not [Partner Platform] shared costs. If [Partner Platform] can cover something, great — but we can't depend on it. See Tool Stack (09) for the full list.

---

## 3. Client Priority vs Personal Brand

**What happened:** [Your Mentor/Advisor] noted in my EOD feedback: "Take care of clients first, since without them, we have no revenue. Selecting swag and working on our personal brands is alluring, but retention is far more important."

**Root cause:** Fair point. I was splitting time between revenue-generating client work and personal brand building. When you're running a small agency, client delivery has to come first — the personal brand compounds over time, but only if clients are happy enough to stay.

**Fix:** Use the Delegation Framework (05). Personal brand content production goes to [Your Content Specialist]. I focus on client delivery and sales. My personal brand gets built from client results and case studies, not from separate content creation sessions.

---

## 4. [Your Content Specialist] Hire Qualification Pushback

**What happened:** [Your Mentor/Advisor] pushed back on [Your Content Specialist] being hired without going through the standard qualifying process (repurpose a Marketing Mechanic video). Then [Your Mentor/Advisor] and [Your Ops Partner] flagged AI language in [Your Content Specialist]'s initial article.

**Root cause:** I hired a friend quickly because I needed help. [Your Mentor/Advisor] was right to push back — qualifying standards exist for a reason, and skipping them created a quality risk that immediately showed up.

**Fix:** Going forward, every hire completes a qualifying task reviewed by [Your Mentor/Advisor]/[Your Ops Partner] before starting paid work. No exceptions. For [Your Content Specialist] specifically: his quality is being monitored closely for 60 days with explicit benchmarks. This is documented in the Scaling Plan (13) as a non-negotiable hiring standard.

---

## 5. Financial Transparency Gap

**What happened:** [Your Mentor/Advisor] noted that I initially didn't share compensation details. He's been pushing for formal P&L reporting and expense accounting before any distributions happen.

**Root cause:** No formal financial reporting system was in place. Revenue flows to me first, then gets distributed — but without clear documentation, that creates trust issues even when everything is above board.

**Fix:** [Your Ops Admin] produces a monthly P&L per the KP Sprint doc structure. I share financials quarterly with equity holders per the information rights clause in the equity agreement. Transparency eliminates questions before they become friction.

---

## 6. Email vs Basecamp Drift

**What happened:** I've been forwarding tasks to [Your Content Specialist] via email instead of assigning them in Basecamp. [Your Mentor/Advisor] has repeatedly asked the team to move discussions to Basecamp.

**Root cause:** Email is faster for quick delegation. But it creates information silos — [Your Mentor/Advisor] and [Your Ops Partner] can't see what [Your Content Specialist] is working on, blockers don't get flagged in the right place, and tasks fall through the cracks.

**Fix:** All task assignments go through Basecamp. Email is only for credentials and 1:1 coordination that doesn't need team visibility. I need to add [Your Content Specialist] to relevant Basecamp projects and assign tasks there. This is part of the Communication Protocol (02).

---

## 7. Refund Request ([Client Name])

**What happened:** [Client Name] emailed asking about a [$Amount] refund. [Your Ops Admin] looped the team.

**Root cause:** The specifics vary, but the bigger issue is that we need clear, documented refund policy enforcement so these don't become ad hoc decisions every time.

**Fix:** The 7-day cooling off policy is now documented in client agreements. Process: [Your Ops Admin] handles initial inquiry, escalates to me + [Your Mentor/Advisor] if unresolved, we decide together. See Escalation Playbook (12) for the full refund workflow.

---

## 8. My Own Low Output

**What happened:** I wrote in February 2026: "It applies to me because my output has been so poor, that no matter how good the tool is, there will be no result without action. I am working hard to change that."

**Root cause:** Multiple priorities pulling in different directions — military service obligations, onboarding [Your Content Specialist], building systems, personal matters. Spread thin across too many things, executing on too few.

**Fix:** The delegation framework is the answer. [Your Content Specialist] handles content production. [Your Ops Admin] handles admin. The PRISM handles systems documentation. My time allocation should be: 60% client delivery, 20% sales, 20% strategic. Everything else gets delegated or deferred.

---

## 9. Google Ads Policy Rejections

**What happened:** Multiple ads got disapproved by Google Ads policy. Time wasted on revisions and resubmissions.

**Root cause:** Ad copy and targeting weren't being reviewed against Google's policies before submission. We were learning by rejection instead of by preparation.

**Fix:** Review all ad copy against Google's current policies before submitting. Assign someone ([Your Content Specialist] or a future content writer) to monitor ad account health weekly. Build a pre-submission checklist based on the most common rejection reasons we've hit.

---

## 10. Shared Tool Access Fragility

**What happened:** The Jumper Media dashboard had an unknown admin. Nobody on the team could identify who set it up. The entire team did a "not me" email chain — nobody claimed it.

**Root cause:** No centralized documentation of who owns what tool, who has admin access, and what credentials exist. When tools are set up ad hoc and people leave or forget, access becomes a mystery.

**Fix:** The Tool Stack document (09) now includes admin/owner info for every tool. [Your Ops Admin] maintains a master access registry. Any time a new tool is added or access changes, it gets updated immediately. No more "who set this up?" conversations.

---

---

## 11. No CRM or Deal Tracking

**What happened:** Client and prospect information lives in scattered emails and Basecamp projects. No centralized pipeline view.

**Root cause:** No system was set up for tracking prospects through qualification to close. Tasks and follow-ups get lost.

**Fix:** Build a lightweight deal tracker in Basecamp or a spreadsheet. Track prospect name, stage, next action, owner, and deadline. Prospect Follow-Up SOP (now created) provides the cadence.

---

## 12. No Content Publishing Calendar

**What happened:** Articles, podcasts, and social content are produced but there is no visible publishing schedule or distribution tracking.

**Root cause:** The pipeline optimized for production volume without a corresponding system for scheduling and verifying publication.

**Fix:** Create a publishing calendar that tracks what was produced, when it was published, and on which platform. Ties into the WordPress API gap identified in the honest assessment.

---

## 13. No Engagement Monitoring for AI Apprentices

**What happened:** Variable participation across apprentices (Alea slow, Morgan inactive) with no systematic check-in cadence or engagement triggers.

**Root cause:** Onboarding exists but ongoing engagement monitoring does not. Apprentices drift without structured accountability.

**Fix:** Weekly engagement check: did they submit work? Did they attend Thursday sessions? If two consecutive misses, trigger outreach. AI Apprentice Onboarding SOP now exists but needs an ongoing engagement section.

---

## Pattern Summary

Most of these pain points trace back to three root causes:

1. **Unclear boundaries between [Your Agency] and [Methodology Partner]/[Partner Platform].** Fix: Document [Your Agency]'s independence, own our tools, own our reporting.
2. **[Your Name] doing too many things.** Fix: Delegation framework, hire a PM, focus on highest-value work.
3. **Communication happening in the wrong channels.** Fix: Basecamp for everything, email for nothing operational.

Fix these three things and 80% of the friction disappears.
