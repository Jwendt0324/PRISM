You're not writing blog posts. You're building someone's digital reputation. Treat it that way.

## Common Mistakes New Hires Make

I'm telling you these upfront so you don't make them. Not because I think you will — because everyone does until someone tells them not to.

### 1. Using AI-Generated Language
[Your Mentor/Advisor] and [Your Ops Partner] will catch it every time. "In today's competitive landscape," "leveraging cutting-edge solutions," "passionate about excellence" — that's bot language. Write like a journalist interviewing a real person. Use the owner's actual words from the transcript. Be specific. Be concrete. If you can't point to a sentence in the transcript that supports what you wrote, delete it.

### 2. Not Updating Basecamp Threads
Everything goes in Basecamp. Not email. Not text. Not "I'll update it later." If you finished a task, mark it done in Basecamp. If you're blocked, post it in Basecamp. If you have a question about a project, post it in the project thread. Email gets lost. Basecamp doesn't.

### 3. Waiting for Me to Assign Work
After Week 1, you should be looking at the content pipeline and pulling tasks yourself. If there are transcripts ready to be written, write them. If there's video ready to be cut, cut it. Don't sit in your inbox waiting for me to tell you what to do. The pipeline is visible. Use it.

### 4. Not Submitting EOD Reports Daily
This is non-negotiable. Every day you work, you post an EOD in Basecamp. What you did, what's blocked, what's next. Takes 5 minutes. Skipping it tells me you're either not working or not organized enough to track your own output. Neither is acceptable.

### 5. Treating Feedback as Criticism
When [Your Mentor/Advisor] rewrites your headline or [Your Ops Partner] flags a paragraph, they're not attacking you. They're teaching you. The people who grow fastest here are the ones who take feedback, implement it immediately, and don't make the same mistake twice. Don't get defensive. Get better.
