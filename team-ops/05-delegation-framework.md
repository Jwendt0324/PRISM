# Delegation Framework

This is the rule book for what I do and what I don't do. If something's on the "delegate" list and I'm still doing it, someone needs to call me on it. The whole point of building a team is to stop being the bottleneck.

[Your Mentor/Advisor] said it plainly: take care of clients first, since without them, we have no revenue. Selecting swag and working on personal brands is alluring, but retention is far more important. That's the filter. Everything below flows from that.

## The Handoff Protocol

**This is the part that fixes the bottleneck.** I have a habit of forwarding emails to [Your Content Specialist] with minimal context. That's lazy delegation and it creates confusion, rework, and delays. Here's how it works now:

### Step 1: Brief in Basecamp (NOT Email)

Every task goes in Basecamp as a to-do assigned to [Your Content Specialist] (or whoever owns it). Not an email forward. Not a text. Basecamp.

**Why:** Email forwards die in inboxes. Basecamp tasks are tracked, visible to the whole team, and have comment threads. [Your Mentor/Advisor] has been saying this. He's right.

### Step 2: Every Brief Includes These Five Things

```
1. WHAT'S NEEDED
   [Clear description of the deliverable. Not "handle this" — actual specifics.]

2. DEADLINE
   [Specific date and time. "ASAP" is not a deadline.]

3. QUALITY STANDARD
   [Link to the relevant QA doc or example of what "done" looks like.
    For articles: link to Article QA SOP.
    For design: link to an approved example.]

4. CONTEXT
   [Why this matters. Who it's for. What it connects to.]

5. WHO TO ASK IF STUCK
   [Not always me. Could be [Your Mentor/Advisor] for content quality, [Your Ops Partner] for process,
    [Your Ops Admin] for tool access, [Your Site Admin] for technical questions.]
```

### Step 3: [Your Content Specialist] Reviews [Your Mentor/Advisor]'s Feedback Patterns

[Your Content Specialist] needs to internalize the quality bar. That means:

- Reading [Your Mentor/Advisor]'s feedback on past content (it's all in Basecamp and email threads)
- Understanding the common flags: AI-sounding language, missing specificity, lack of personal voice
- Reviewing the Article QA & Compliance SOP before every writing task
- When in doubt, asking [Your Ops Partner] to gut-check before submitting to me for final QA

### Step 4: I Review Output, Not Process

I don't need to know how [Your Content Specialist] made it. I need to see the finished product and confirm it meets the quality bar. If it does, it ships. If it doesn't, I give specific feedback (not "make it better" — actual notes on what to fix).

## The Email Forwarding Problem — Solved

**Old way:**
1. I get an email about something
2. I forward it to [Your Content Specialist] with "can you handle this"
3. No one else sees it
4. No deadline
5. No quality standard
6. [Your Content Specialist] guesses what I want
7. I end up redoing half of it

**New way:**
1. I get an email about something
2. I create a Basecamp to-do with the five-part brief
3. I assign it to [Your Content Specialist] (or whoever owns it)
4. I attach the email or relevant content as context
5. The whole team can see it
6. [Your Content Specialist] knows exactly what's needed and when
7. It comes back right the first time

**Time saved:** 5 minutes to write a proper brief now saves 30+ minutes of back-and-forth and rework later.

## Weekly Self-Audit

Every Friday, I check myself against this framework. Five questions:

1. **Did I do anything on the "delegate" list this week?** If yes, why? Fix the handoff so it doesn't happen again.
2. **Did I forward emails to [Your Content Specialist] instead of using Basecamp?** Count them. The number should be zero.
3. **How many hours did I spend on client-facing work vs. admin?** Target: 80% client-facing, 20% everything else.
4. **Did any client deliverable go out late?** If yes, was it because I was the bottleneck on QA?
5. **What did I do this week that [Your Mentor/Advisor] would call "alluring but not important"?** Be honest.
